Tell me about your mess

every one gets a reply

Chaos is the medium. Calm is the method. I'm curious about the mess.

Every practice has one. The workflow nobody will touch. The model that fights back. The template three people understand and one person trusts. The tool the firm bought two years ago and never opened.

That's the mess — and I collect them. Write to me about yours. I read every one, and I reply to every one. The best tangles become future essays, anonymized unless you'd like the credit. That's the deal behind the name: you send me a mess, and everyone gets one less.

How I work

three principles and a log
i.

Create first, critique second.

You cannot critique and create simultaneously. Make the first mark on the page and go from there — design is iterative, and there's only so much you can hold in your head.

ii.

Requirements are an armature, not a mold.

Standards are a framework for achieving more, not a box to stay inside. There are many paths through the requirements — but you cannot ignore them.

iii.

Grade the overrule, not the output.

The question for any AI-polished deliverable: where in this did a human disagree? A review that can't locate the disagreement is reviewing the machine, not the designer.

About

the person behind the margin

Jordan Billingsley is a design technologist who lives for complex puzzles and has zero patience for tedium — which is exactly why he automates it away.

He is Senior Associate and Senior Design Technology Manager at Hord Coplan Macht in Baltimore, adjunct faculty at Morgan State University's School of Architecture & Planning, and a contributor to Superusers (2019) and The Agentic Architect (2026). Trained as an architect at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (B.Arch, M.Arch).

ARCHIKAOS is his notebook on the space between carefully designed structure and the formless matter before creation — turning disorder into systems, and systems back into possibility. One mess at a time.

<p>Every day, millions of us type some version of the same sentence into AI:</p>
<div class="crescendo"><div class="c1">You are an expert.</div><div class="c2">You are an expert.</div><div class="c3">You are an expert.</div></div>
<p>The strange part is not that the machine answers.<br>The strange part is what that sentence reveals about us.</p>
<p>This is the first of five short essays, one for each word of the title. Together they make a single argument: the machine speaks with the voice of everyone. The question is whether we can still answer as someone.</p>
<p>Starting with the first word.</p>
<p>Every prompt begins by imagining a counterpart.</p>
<div class="qstack">"You are a mentor who has seen this mistake before."<br>"You are a critic who cares more about the work than my feelings."<br>"You are a collaborator who challenges my assumptions."</div>
<p>The "you" may not be a person, but it is certainly not nothing. It creates the frame for the exchange. It names the kind of intelligence, resistance, or help we hope to meet on the other side.</p>
<p>And in doing so, it reveals us.</p>
<p>Look closely at what we ask it to be. <b><em>Be direct with me. Push back. Don't flatter me. Explain it like I'm smart but new to this.</em></b> Every persona we write is a quiet portrait of what we're reaching for: the colleague we wish we had, the patience we're short on, the honesty we don't always get.</p>
<p>Tell me how you address your machine, and I will tell you something true about you.</p>
<p>In our profession, this should feel familiar. A project brief has never been a neutral document. The way a client describes the architect they want (visionary, listener, problem-solver, magician) tells you half of what they actually need before you've seen the site. The "you" is always a portrait of the author.</p>
<p>But here is the part that should give us pause.</p>
<p>The address runs both ways.</p>
<p>The model was shaped by millions of conversations before yours. It was trained on what people rewarded, refined toward what people approved. When it responds warmly, encouragingly, agreeably, it is not being polite. It is being optimized. It arrives already knowing how to address <em>you</em>.</p>
<p>We think we are the author of the conversation.<br>We walked into a room that was already speaking.</p>
<p>So the first word of the sentence turns out to be a two-way mirror. We summon a counterpart, and the counterpart (assembled from everyone who came before us) summons something in return: our trust, our ease, our agreement.</p>
<p>Which is worth sitting with, because everything that follows in this series flows from it. What we address shapes what answers. And what answers shapes more than the work.</p>
<p>We said "you." We said who it should be.</p>
<p>But the dangerous word in the sentence isn't "you."</p>
<p>It's the next one.</p>
